considered  limited  risk  if  used  to  detect  cosmetic  flaws  in  consumer  goods.  However, if,  for  example,  the  same  technology  is  repurposed  to  identify  structural  defects  in aircraft  parts,  it  likely  becomes  high  risk  due  to  the  safety  implications.

## 2.7 Minimal-Risk  AI  Systems

This  category  includes  AI  applications  deemed  so  unlikely  to  harm  individuals  or society  that  the  Act  imposes  no  specific  legal  obligations  beyond  general  principles of  transparency  and  ethical  design.  At  present  it  is  likely  that  the  vast  majority  of  AI systems  used  in  manufacturing  operations  will  fall  here,  posing  only  negligible  risks. These  face  no  specific  legal  obligations  under  the  Act  aside  from  general  EU  laws. Examples  might  include  AI  that  sorts  incoming  raw  materials  for  inventory  (with no safety impact)  or AI in office productivity  tools,  such as  invoice  matching  or automatic  email  responses.  While  legally  there's  no  action  required,  manufacturers are  encouraged  to  still  follow  ethical  AI  guidelines  (transparency,  data  privacy,  etc.) as  a  matter  of  corporate  responsibility. ## Maintaining  Good  Practices

It's important  to  note  that the classification depends  on  the  use  case, not just the technology  itself.  The  very  same  AI  algorithm  could  be  minimal  risk  in  one  context and  high  risk  in  another.  For  example,  an  image  recognition  AI  is  low  risk  when sorting  parts  on  a  conveyor,  but  if  it's  used  as  a  quality  gate  for  detecting  defects  in airplane  engine  components,  it  could  become  high  risk  due  to  safety  implications (lives  might  depend  on  those  decisions).  Therefore,  manufacturers  must  evaluate each  specific  application  of  AI  in  their  products  or  processes.

Even  when  the  law  does  not  mandate  thorough  documentation,  businesses  are encouraged  to  maintain  good  development  and  quality  control  practices.  Doing  so may  prevent  liability  if  a  system  unexpectedly  transitions  to  a  higher-risk  context  in future.  In  the  dynamic  world  of  industrial  automation,  an  apparently  mundane  AI tool  could  be  later  integrated  into  critical  workflows,  raising  its  risk  profile.

## 2.8 General-Purpose  AI:  An  Emerging  Focus

In  addition  to  the  four  risk  tiers,  there  is  growing  attention  on  general-purpose  AI (GPAI).  Large  language  models,  generative  AI  systems,  and  other  multi-use  technologies  can  be  applied  in  various  sectors  with  varying  risk  outcomes.  While  not formally  categorised  as  a  separate  tier,  the  EU  AI  Act  has  opened  discussions  on how  to  handle  systems  whose  ultimate  applications  may  be  beyond  the  direct  control of  their  original  developers.

For  instance,  a  general-purpose  vision  model  might  be  fine-tuned  for  use  in  either benign  inventory  management  or  invasive  facial  recognition.  The  legislation  may  add further  clarifications  or  obligations,  prompting  developers  of  GPAI  to  share  usage guidelines,  maintain  traceability  of  training  data,  or  implement  usage  restrictions  [5]. Manufacturers  relying  on  GPAI  for  tasks  like  predictive  maintenance  must  monitor how  updates  or  modifications  to  underlying  models  affect  risk  classification.

## 2.9 Practical  Examples  in  Manufacturing

The  following  list  provides  a  series  of  10  practical,  real-world  AI  scenarios  identified from  recent  empirical  research.  These  are  grouped  below  into  two  main  categories: (1)  manufacturing-based  embedded  AI  use  cases  and  (2)  product-based  embedded AI  use  cases.

## Group  1:  Manufacturing  Embedded  AI

## 1. Predictive  Maintenance  in  High-Precision  Manufacturing

AI  algorithms  continuously  monitor  real-time  sensor  data  from  high-precision  manufacturing  equipment  to  identify  potential  failures  before  they  occur.  These  systems directly  impact machinery  used  in critical sectors, such as aerospace  or  medical device  production,  where  errors  can  lead  to  safety  concerns  or  regulatory  violations.

## 2. Autonomous  Robotics  for  Assembly  Lines

AI  controlled  robots  execute  repetitive  assembly  tasks,  such  as  welding,  painting, or assembling components, in a controlled manufacturing environment. These systems  are designed  with minimal  human  interaction  and risk of harm, relying on  adaptive  decision-making  to  respond  to  anomalies  in  real  time  and  correct  errors autonomously.

## 3. AI-Driven  Quality  Control  in  Medical  Device  Production

Computer vision-powered AI systems are deployed to detect micro-defects in medical  device  production.  These  systems  ensure  compliance  with  stringent  quality standards  to  prevent  defective  products  from  entering  the  market,  which  could  pose risks  to  patient  health  and  safety.

## 4. AI  for  Energy  Optimisation  in  Smart  Factories

AI  algorithms  dynamically  manage  energy  consumption  by  monitoring  and  adjusting machinery  and  HVAC  systems  in  real  time  to  reduce  waste  and  improve  energy  efficiency.  These  systems  indirectly  contribute  to  sustainability  goals  without  impacting human  safety.

## 5. Digital  Twin  for  Factory  Operations

AI-driven  digital  twins  replicate  physical  manufacturing  processes  in  a  virtual  environment.  This  allows  manufacturers  to  test  workflows,  predict  bottlenecks,  and  optimise  production  plans  without  disrupting  the  actual  factory  floor  or  exposing  workers to  physical  risks.

## 6. AI  for  Autonomous  Navigation  in  Vehicles

AI systems control braking, steering, and navigation in semi-autonomous  vehicles.  These  systems  rely  on  real-time  data  from  road  and  the  driver  collected  via sensors and cameras to make split-second decisions in dynamic environments. Some  implementations,  however,  may  use  subliminal  techniques  to  influence  driver behaviour.

## 7. AI-Powered  Battery  Management  for  Electric  Vehicles

AI  systems  monitor  and  optimise  battery  usage,  predict  energy  needs,  and  prevent overheating  or  failure  in  electric  vehicles.  Failures  in  these  systems  could  lead  to catastrophic events, such as battery fires, posing risks to vehicle occupants and surrounding  infrastructure.

## 8. In-Cabin  Monitoring  Systems  in  Autonomous  Vehicles

AI systems analyse driver and passenger behaviours to enhance safety, such as detecting  drowsiness,  distraction,  or  improper  seatbelt  use.  These  systems  provide real-time  alerts  and  can  influence  vehicle  operation  based  on  their  findings.

## 9. AI-Driven  Collision  Avoidance  in  Automotive  Systems

AI-powered  collision  avoidance  systems  use  advanced  sensor  fusion  and  predictive modelling  to  detect  and  respond  to  potential  on-road  collisions  in  vehicles.  These systems  are  critical  for  ensuring  safety  in  dynamic  environments.

## 10. AI  in  Consumer  Robotics  for  Home  Automation

AI-powered  consumer  robots  perform  home  automation  tasks,  such  as  vacuuming. They  primarily  enhance  convenience  and  have  limited  safety  or  ethical  implications.

## 2.10 Challenges  in  Determining  Risk  Levels

While  the  Act  aims  to  provide  clarity,  grey  areas  remain.  Determining  the  boundary between  limited  risk  and  high  risk  can  be  difficult,  particularly  in  industrial  contexts where  the  same  AI  model  can  be  used  in  multiple  ways.  The  following  challenges often  arise:

## · Context  Dependence

A  single  AI  product's  risk  level  can  change  based  on  operational  deployment.  The same  image  recognition  system  might  be  minimal  risk  in  a  benign  environment  yet become  high  risk  in  safety-critical  applications.

## · Evolving  Systems

AI that continues learning post-deployment (online learning models, adaptive systems)  could  initially  pass  compliance  checks  but  later  drift  into  riskier  territory  if updates  alter  its  decision-making.

## · Supply  Chain  Complexities

An  AI  subsystem  integrated  into  a  broader  product  might  appear  low  risk  in  isolation, yet  the  entire  product's  risk  could  be  elevated.  Collaboration  and  due  diligence  with suppliers  are  crucial  to  maintain  compliance.

## · Unforeseen  Emerging  Risks

Rapid  advancements  or  novel  use  cases  can  lead  to  previously  unconsidered  risks. For  instance,  a  data  aggregator  tool  might  inadvertently  collect  sensitive  personal data  from  employees,  raising  privacy  and  ethical  concerns.

To navigate these complexities, manufacturers should adopt conservative approaches,  consult  frequently  with  legal  and  technical  experts,  and  remain  prepared to  reclassify  AI  systems  if  new  information  suggests  a  higher-risk  category.

## 2.11 Aligning  Risk  Categories  with  Compliance  Strategy

The  main  takeaway  for  organisations  is that  every  AI  system  should  undergo  an internal  review  or  'risk  classification  check.'  This  process  could  be  implemented into the engineering  process flow for developing  new  products  or processes and should  typically  involve:

## · Mapping  Functionalities

Document  the  AI  system's  purpose,  scope,  and  control  over  critical  processes  or decisions.

## · Assessing  Potential  Harms

Consider worst-case scenarios, paying attention to safety, health, privacy, or fundamental  rights.  Where  these  harms  appear  severe,  a  higher-risk  tier  may  apply.

## · Reviewing  Annexes  and  Guidelines

Consult  the  official  annexes  (particularly  Annex  III)  and  any  sector-specific  regulatory  guidance  to  clarify  categories.

## · Determining  Compliance  Measures

High-risk  systems  necessitate  thorough  documentation,  external  audits  (if  required), and  post-market  checks.  Limited-risk  and  minimal-risk  systems  have  fewer  obligations  but  still  benefit  from  good  design  practices  and  ethical  considerations.

By  methodically  implementing  this  classification  step,  manufacturers  can  avoid last-minute  surprises.  A  well-structured  risk  analysis  also  lays  a  solid  foundation  for subsequent  chapters'  topics,  such  as  data  governance,  transparency,  and  post-market monitoring  obligations.

## 2.12 Conclusion

Risk  classification  is  the  linchpin  of  the  EU  AI  Act,  guiding  organisations  in  how intensively  they  must  prepare  for  compliance.  Whether  an  AI  tool  is  deemed  prohibited,  high  risk,  limited  risk,  or  minimal  risk  depend  largely  on  the  potential  impact  on individual  safety,  fundamental  rights,  and  societal  well-being.  Manufacturers  operating  in  or  selling  to  the  EU  must  be  thorough  in  categorising  their  AI  systems,  for misclassification  could  result  in  significant  legal  liabilities  or  the  forced  withdrawal of  non-compliant  products  from  the  market.

A  few  major  points  bear  repeating:

- Definition  of  AI :  Incredibly  broad,  capturing  nearly  any  software  that  infers  or decides,  irrespective  of  the  underlying  technique.
- Unacceptable/Prohibited  Systems : No  scope  for legal compliance;  these  are simply  banned.
- High  Risk :  The  most  stringent  requirements,  covering  critical  applications  that could  severely  affect  safety  or  rights.
- Limited  Risk :  Lighter  obligations,  mainly  transparency,  though  systems  can  be reclassified  if  circumstances  change.
- Minimal Risk : Few or no explicit requirements, yet best practices remain advisable.
- General-Purpose  AI :  Though  not  a  separate  tier,  concerns  around  multi-use  or large-scale  models  could  expand  obligations  for  developers  and  adopters  alike.

In  the  chapters  that  follow,  we  will  delve  deeper  into  how  these  risk  tiers  influence the  specific  compliance  obligations,  starting  with  a  global  perspective  in  Chap.  3, where  we  discuss  how  non-EU  manufacturers  can  manage  cross-border  compliance  challenges  under  the  EU  AI  Act.  Through  this  lens,  we  will  also  explore  how

different  regulatory  frameworks  around  the  world  affect  (and  are  affected  by)  the EU's  leadership  role  in  AI  governance.

## Key  Takeaways

- I. The  EU  AI  Act  covers  most  AI  techniques,  ensuring  new  technologies  do  not sidestep  regulatory  oversight.
- II. From  prohibited  to  minimal,  the  Act  enforces  proportionate  obligations  based on  potential  harm.
- III. High-risk  systems,  common  in  manufacturing,  involve  strict  requirements  on data,  documentation,  human  oversight,  accuracy,  and  post-market  monitoring.
- IV. Lower  tiers  face  fewer  legal  obligations,  though  transparency  and  good  design practices  remain  essential.
- V. An  AI's classification depends  on its use case and environment;  the  same system can move from limited to high risk if deployed in safety-critical scenarios.
- VI. High-risk  systems  may  need  thorough  internal  or  external  audits.
- VII. The classification landscape may evolve as AI technologies develop, so ongoing  vigilance  is  required.

Armed  with  this  foundational  knowledge,  manufacturers  can  more  effectively chart  their  compliance  strategies  under  the  EU  AI  Act  and  avoid  pitfalls  that  arise from  misunderstanding  the  Act's  definitions  and  risk  categories.

## Chapter  3 Global  Implications  for  Non-EU Manufacturers

Abstract Although  the  EU  AI  Act  is  European  legislation,  its  extraterritorial  reach compels  non-EU  firms  to  comply  whenever  their  AI-enabled  products  or  outputs reach  the  Union.  This  chapter  analyses  the  Act's  global  ripple  effects,  compares  it  with parallel  frameworks  in  the  United  States  and  Asia,  and  outlines  practical  strategies  for navigating  overlapping  regimes.  Case  vignettes  demonstrate  how  supply  chain  partners,  distributors,  and  local  subsidiaries  can  trigger  EU  jurisdiction.  Readers  learn  to anticipate  compliance  pressures,  leverage  early  alignment  as  a  market  differentiator, and  prepare  for  future  interoperability  or  divergence  across  regions.

## 3.1 Introduction

The European  Union  (EU)  has a storied history of setting influential regulatory precedents,  and  the  EU  Artificial  Intelligence  (AI)  Act  is  no  exception.  Although  this legislation  originates  within  the  EU,  its  effects  extend  far  beyond  European  borders. Any  manufacturer  whose  AI systems reach the EU  market  or  impact  EU-based individuals  fall  under  the  Act's  jurisdiction,  regardless  of  where  that  manufacturer is  headquartered.  In  today's  interconnected  world  of  global  supply  chains  and  crossborder  data  flows,  the  practical  consequence  is  that  countless  non-EU  businesses  are now  compelled  to  adopt  EU  standards  for  their  AI  products  and  services.

This  chapter  delves  into  what  the  EU  AI  Act  means  for  manufacturers  and  engineers  located  outside  the  EU.  We  begin  by  exploring  the  Act's  extraterritorial  scope, which  underpins  its  global  reach.  We  then  examine  the  compliance  pressures  driving non-EU  entities  to  align  with  EU  rules,  often  independently  of  any  formal  enforcement  mechanism  in  their  home  countries.  Following  that,  we  discuss  cross-border compliance  strategies  and  the  steps  non-EU  manufacturers  can  take  to  adapt  their processes,  supply  chains,  and  governance  frameworks.  We  also  explore  how  the  Act interacts  with  other  regulatory  frameworks  worldwide,  from  the  United  States  to China,  highlighting  both  divergences  and  areas  for  possible  convergence.

By  the  end  of  this  chapter,  you  should  understand  not  only  why  non-EU  manufacturers  need  to  worry  about  EU  AI  law  but  also  how  they  can  proactively  meet  its

demands.  This  knowledge  is  crucial  for  any  enterprise  keen  on  retaining  access  to  the lucrative  European  market  and  fostering  a  reputation  for  responsible  AI  innovation on  the  global  stage.

## 3.2 The  Extraterritorial  Scope  of  the  EU  AI  Act

A  hallmark  of  modern  EU  regulations,  starting  perhaps  most  prominently  with  the General  Data  Protection  Regulation  (GDPR),  is  their  reach  beyond  the  borders  of EU  Member  States.  The  EU  AI  Act  follows  this  blueprint.  Article  2(1)  stipulates  that the  Act  applies  to:

1. Providers offering  AI  systems  in  the  EU,  whether  they  are  established  within the  EU  or  in  a  third  country.
2. Users of  AI  systems  within  the  EU.
3. Providers  and  users located  in  a  third  country,  if  the  output  produced  by  their AI  system  is  used  within  the  EU.

For example, a Canadian firm producing AI-enabled robotics distributed to Germany  must  ensure  its  products  comply  with  the  Act  because  they  are  ultimately utilised  in  an  EU  context.

This extraterritorial application drives non-EU  manufacturers  to integrate  EU standards into global product designs, reducing complexity and mitigating legal conflicts,  the  phenomenon  known  as  the  'Brussels  Effect'  [1].

## Who  Is  Affected?

From  a  manufacturing  standpoint,  any  company  producing  AI-enhanced  machinery, robotics, or software  that is sold or  utilised  in  EU  Member  States  must  comply. Similarly, if the organisation  sells its products  to a third  party  who  then  imports those  products  into  the  EU,  the  Act's  obligations  also  filter  through  the  supply  chain.

Example :  A  Canadian  firm  produces  AI-enabled  robotics  for  industrial  warehouses. Although  the  robots  are  assembled  in  Canada,  they  are  sold  to  distributors  worldwide,  including  in  Germany.  Because  these  robots  are  eventually  deployed  in  an  EU warehouse  environment,  the  Canadian  firm's  AI  components  must  meet  the  EU  AI Act's  requirements.

## Implications  for  Corporate  Strategy

The  extraterritorial  application  prompts  many  non-EU  manufacturers  to  revise  their internal  AI  governance  models.  Compliance  can  no  longer  be  an  afterthought  or  an 'EU-only'  affair.  Instead,  integrating  the  EU  AI  Act's  principles  into  global  product design ensures efficiency, consistency, and reduced  liability, rather than juggling multiple  versions  of  the  same  product  for  different  markets.

Moreover,  non-EU  businesses  may  find  themselves  compelled  to  adopt  EU  standards  universally,  a  phenomenon  termed  the  'Brussels  Effect.'  By  standardising  on

the  EU's  benchmark,  global  manufacturers  can  reduce  complexity  and  potential  legal conflicts  across  multiple  jurisdictions.

## 3.3 Compliance  Pressures  Beyond  Regulatory  Enforcement

A  common  question  is  how  the  EU  AI  Act  can  practically  enforce  compliance  against an  enterprise  situated  thousands  of  miles  away,  outside  EU  boundaries.  While  direct enforcement  might  pose  certain  challenges,  several  indirect  but  potent  forces  ensure compliance.

## The  Role  of  Market  Access

The  EU's  single  market  is  one  of  the  largest  and  most  lucrative  in  the  world.  Many global manufacturers cannot afford to lose access. If the EU  AI Act bars  noncompliant  products,  or  if  fines  and  injunctions  threaten  a  manufacturer's  dealings with  EU  partners,  the  potential  financial  damage  is  considerable  [2].  Consequently, fear  of  losing  a  significant  market  share  incentivises  alignment  with  EU  rules.

Example :  A  Brazilian  manufacturer  of  AI-powered  diagnostic  equipment  may  have a small but growing  client base in Spain. Non-compliance  with  the EU  AI Act could  lead  to  product  bans,  forfeiture  of  potential  sales,  and  reputational  harm,  thus undermining  the  company's  European  ambitions.

## Supply  Chain  Requirements

Even  if a  manufacturer  has no direct contracts in EU  states, it might be part of a broader  supply  chain  that ends up selling  AI-integrated  products  in  Europe.  In such cases, downstream compliance obligations transfer upstream. Distributors, importers, or EU-based  firms may  insist that suppliers certify their AI  modules meet  EU  standards.  Failure  to  provide  documentation  such  as  risk  assessments,  data governance  proof,  or  conformity  certificates  may  result  in  lost  business  opportunities [3].

## Reputational  Considerations

Increasingly,  stakeholders  and  customers  worldwide  are  conscious  of  ethical  and safety standards. Aligning with the EU  AI Act, given its focus on transparency and risk mitigation, can enhance  a  manufacturer's  global  reputation.  Conversely, being  publicly  found  non-compliant  can  tarnish  a  brand's  credibility,  even  in  regions without  stringent  AI  laws  [4].

## 3.4 Navigating  Cross-Border  Compliance  Challenges

Understanding  the  rationale  behind  the  EU  AI  Act  is  one  thing;  translating  that  into day-to-day  operations  across  different  continents  is  another.  Non-EU  manufacturers often  grapple  with  logistical,  legal,  and  cultural  hurdles.

## Data  Transfers  and  Governance

One  frequent  issue  is  where  and  how  data  is  stored,  processed,  or  analysed.  The  EU AI  Act  dovetails  with  broader  EU  data  privacy  and  security  norms,  including  the GDPR.  This  interplay  becomes  particularly  relevant  if  an  AI  system  requires  data from  EU-based  users  or  sensors.  Manufacturers  must  ensure  that:

1. Data  is  collected  lawfully.
2. Adequate  measures  are  in  place  to  avoid  bias,  as  mandated  by  Article  10  (data governance).
3. Personal  data,  if  any,  is  protected  in  line  with  GDPR  or  equivalent  frameworks.

Cross-Border  Implications :  A  factory  in  South  Korea  capturing  real-time  performance  data  from  machines  operating  in  Poland  must  ensure  that not only is the machine's  AI  module  compliant  under  the  EU  AI  Act  but  that  data  handling  meets relevant  EU  data  protection  standards.

## Conformity  Assessments  from  Abroad

High-risk  AI  systems  may  require  formal  conformity  assessments  (Article  43)  to demonstrate  compliance  before  being  placed  on  the  EU  market.  For  non-EU  manufacturers, this  typically  involves  working  with  a  notified  body  recognised  by  EU authorities.  The  complexity  here  lies  in:

Logistics :  Arranging  for  prototypes  or  documentation  to  be  reviewed,  potentially involving  the  shipping  of  sample  products  or  remote  auditing.

Documentation  Gaps :  Non-EU  firms  may  lack  the  precise  format  or  level  of  detail demanded  by  EU  authorities.

To  streamline  the  process,  many  businesses  designate  an  'Authorised  Representative'  within  the  EU  who  liaises  with  notified  bodies  and  regulators,  ensuring  local presence  and  quicker  response  times  [5].

## Language  and  Cultural  Barriers

EU regulations often require user manuals, risk assessments, or disclaimers in one or more official EU languages. A manufacturer in Thailand exporting  AIintegrated  machining  tools  to  Germany  and  France  must  provide  accurate  translations and  ensure  that  the  instructions  meet  local  language  and  cultural  expectations  [6]. Miscommunication  can  lead  to  user  misinterpretation  of  safety  procedures,  which  in turn  could  violate  the  Act's  transparency  or  oversight  clauses.

## Legal  Overlaps  and  Conflicts

Some  countries  outside  the  EU  may  have  their  own  AI-related  legislation,  or  sectoral laws  that  conflict  with  certain  EU  provisions.  In  the  United  States,  for  example,  no single  overarching  federal  AI  law  exists,  but  numerous  agencies  provide  guidance, creating  a  patchwork  of  norms.  A  non-EU  manufacturer  may  need  to  reconcile:

- EU  demands  for  human  oversight  with  local  norms  that  emphasise  a  'light-touch' approach  to  AI.
- EU  emphasis  on  ethical  design  with  local  or  regional  regulations  that  lack  explicit ethical  guidelines.

The  challenge  is  more  acute  for  multinational  firms  operating  in  multiple  jurisdictions.  They  might  need  to  create  multi-tiered  compliance  structures  or,  conversely, adopt  the  strictest  common  denominator,  often  the  EU's  standard.

## 3.5 Comparing  the  EU  AI  Act  with  Other  Regulatory Frameworks

To  better  appreciate  the  global  implications,  it  helps  to  contrast  the  EU  AI  Act  with other  prominent  regulatory  approaches  around  the  world.

## The  United  States

Regulatory  Landscape :  The  US  primarily  relies  on  sector-specific  guidelines,  e.g. the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for medical devices or the National Highway  Traffic  Safety  Administration  (NHTSA)  for  autonomous  vehicles.

Federal  Initiatives :  There  have  been  discussions  around  an  AI  Bill  of  Rights  and voluntary  frameworks  led  by  the  National  Institute  of  Standards  and  Technology (NIST).  However,  no  single  federal  law  comparable  to  the  EU  AI  Act  exists.

Key  Differences :  US  regulators  tend  to  focus  on  innovation  and  competitiveness, with  an  emphasis  on  self-regulation.  The  EU  AI  Act,  by  contrast,  is  more  prescriptive, mandating  audits  and  risk  mitigation  steps  [1].

For  a  non-EU  manufacturer  aiming  to  sell  AI-powered  medical  robots  both  in  the United  States  and  Europe,  reconciling  the  US's  less  centralised  approach  with  the EU's  structured,  risk-based  framework  can  be  challenging.  Nevertheless,  meeting EU  standards  often  positions  the  product  well  for  acceptance  in  many  US  contexts, though  additional  clarifications  might  still  be  required  for  FDA  approval.

## China

Centralised Oversight : China's government actively shapes AI development, emphasising  national  security,  social  stability,  and  economic  competitiveness.

Recent  Regulations :  Provisions  around  algorithmic  transparency,  data  security,  and content  management  have  been  introduced,  often  focusing  on  social  media  and  data privacy.

Key  Differences :  While  the  EU  approach  centres  on  fundamental  rights  and  risk mitigation,  China's  AI  governance  is  shaped  heavily  by  public  security  objectives. For  example,  social  credit  systems  are  widely  used  there,  a  practice  the  EU  AI  Act explicitly  prohibits.

A  non-EU  manufacturer  dealing  with  China  faces  quite  a  different  environment from  that  of  the  EU.  In  effect,  it  must  navigate  two  distinct  sets  of  rules,  leading  some global  enterprises  to  adopt  separate  product  strategies  per  region.

## The  United  Kingdom

Post-Brexit  Divergence :  The  UK  is  formulating  its  own  AI  regulatory  approach, which  is  generally  more  'light-touch'  and  principle-based  compared  to  the  EU's prescriptive  model.

Sector  Regulators :  The  UK  government  envisages  that  existing  regulators-such  as those  in  health,  finance,  or  transport-will  provide  AI  guidance  within  their  domains, rather  than  introducing  a  single  AI  law.

Key  Differences :  While  many  UK  rules  may  remain  compatible  with  or  influenced by  the  EU  approach,  there  is  potential  for  divergence  over  time.

UK-based manufacturers selling  to  the  EU  must  still  comply  with  EU  rules  for  their exports.  Conversely,  domestic  UK  regulations  might  differ,  creating  dual  compliance  burdens  or,  in  some  cases,  potential  advantage  if  the  UK  offers  less  stringent compliance  obligations.

## 3.6 Strategies  for  Non-EU  Manufacturers

Despite  these  complexities,  non-EU  manufacturers  can  implement  several  proven strategies  to  align  themselves  with  the  EU  AI  Act.

## Conduct  a  Comprehensive  AI  Inventory

Start  by  identifying  which  AI  systems  within  your  product  lines  interact  with  EUbased  users  or  markets.  For  each  system,  document  its  function,  data  sources,  and potential  risks  (as  per  the  risk  classification  framework  discussed  in  Chap.  2).  This step  underpins  all  subsequent  compliance  decisions.
