## A Note on Moving Goalposts

Regulators,  like  engineers,  work  with  tolerances,  but  the  political  and  technological tolerances  around  AI  are  currently  measured  in  weeks,  not  microns.  As  we  wrap  up this  manuscript  (mid-2025),  Brussels  is  still  debating  delegated  acts,  Washington  is  as unpredictable  as  ever  and  at  least  three  large  language  models  have  broken  the  FLOP (floating  point  operations  per  second)  speed  record  since  you  started  this  paragraph. In  other  words,  the  insights  and  technical  information  you  will  find  on  the  pages ahead  are  accurate  at  the  time  of  printing,  yet  despite  being  fundamental  principles enshrined  in  law,  they  sit  on  shifting  foundations.  Think  of  them  as  a  well-calibrated digital  twin  of  today's  landscape  rather  than  a  static  CNC  drawing  guaranteed  to  fit tomorrow's  parts.

Why  mention  this?  Because good engineering practice  demands  documented assumptions.  The  core  assumption  here  is  that  Regulation  (EU)  2024/1689  remains materially  unchanged  and  that  the  accompanying  ISO/IEC  standards  progress  along their  current  trajectories.  Should  a  geopolitical  tremor,  a  breakthrough  in  neuromorphic  chips,  or  an  unexpected  parliamentary  amendment  alter  that  path,  you  may  need to  redline  a  section  or  two,  preferably  before  your  next  conformity  assessment.

So,  please  read  the  book  with  a  screwdriver  in  one  hand  and  a  version-control  tag in  the  other.  We  have  done  our  best  to  future-proof  the  guidance,  but  just  like  any predictive-maintenance  model,  it  will  require  fine-tuning  as  new  data  arrive.  Until then,  torque  these  insights  to  spec,  keep  an  eye  on  the  dashboard,  and  enjoy  the  build.

## Chapter 1 The EU AI Act-Foundations and Framework

Abstract This  chapter  explains  why  the  European  Union  introduced  a  dedicated Artificial  Intelligence  Act  and  situates  the  regulation  within  the  EU's  broader  policy landscape.  It  contrasts  the  Act's  aims:  Trust,  innovation,  and  protection  of  fundamental  rights,  with  earlier  cornerstone  legislation  such  as  the  GDPR,  highlighting both continuities and novel elements (e.g. the risk-based tiers for AI systems). Readers  gain a concise overview  of the Act's structure, key articles,  and  implementation  timeline,  together  with  an  introduction  to  the  'Brussels  Effect,'  which helps  EU  norms  shape  global  practice.  By  the  end  of  the  chapter,  manufacturers  and engineers  understand  the  legal  foundations  they  must  work  with  and  the  strategic rationale  that  underpins  the  new  rules.

## 1.1 Introduction

The  European  Union  (EU)  has  taken  decisive  steps  to  regulate  the  rapidly  evolving field  of  artificial  intelligence  (AI).  In  doing  so,  it  seeks  not  only  to  protect  fundamental rights  and  consumer  safety  but  also  to  establish  clear  rules  that  encourage  responsible innovation.  This  chapter  explores  the  foundational  rationale  behind  the  EU  AI  Act,  its overarching  objectives,  and  how  it  aligns  with  or  diverges  from  previous  legislative efforts  such  as  the  General  Data  Protection  Regulation  (GDPR).  We  will  also  examine its  extraterritorial  reach,  timeline,  and  its  significance  for  both  EU-based  and  global organisations.

This  journey  begins  with  understanding  the  motivations  of  the  EU  in  embarking on  such  a  pioneering  legislative  effort.  As  with  the  GDPR,  the  EU  AI  Act  embodies a  vision  in  which  regulatory  oversight  goes  hand  in  hand  with  industrial  competitiveness  and  protection  of  human  values.  For  businesses,  especially  those  in  manufacturing  and  engineering,  gaining  clarity  on  why  the  EU  enacted  this  law  is  a  valuable first  step  towards  effective  compliance.  By  the  end  of  this  chapter,  readers  will  grasp how  the  EU  AI  Act  lays  the  groundwork  for  a  new  era  in  AI  governance,  outlining both  responsibilities  and  opportunities  for  those  looking  to  harness  the  power  of  AI responsibly.

## 1.2 The Case for AI Regulation

AI  has  gradually  permeated  almost  every  sector  of  the  economy,  reshaping  processes in  ways  that  were  unthinkable  a  decade  ago.  Manufacturers  deploy  machine  learning models  to  streamline  supply  chains  and  automate  quality  control;  financial  services use  AI-driven  tools  for  fraud  detection  and  credit  assessment;  healthcare  systems rely  on  predictive  analytics  for  diagnosis  support.  With  this  widespread  adoption, however,  has  come  a  parallel  rise in  concerns:  questions  about  fairness  and  bias, the  opacity  of  so-called  black-box  models,  the  reliability  of  safety-critical  AI  applications  and  the  broader  ethical  implications  of  widespread  AI-based  automation  in decision  systems.

## Evolving Public Perception

Public  opinion  on  AI  has  fluctuated  between  excitement  and  wariness.  On  one  hand, AI  is  championed  for  its  potential  to  boost  productivity  and  spur  economic  growth. On  the other, stories of job displacement,  biased  facial recognition  systems,  and intrusive  surveillance  have  fuelled  scepticism  and  calls  for  stronger  oversight.  The EU,  noting  these  developments,  resolved  that  it  was  time  to  set  out  a  unifying  set  of rules  that  would  guide  AI  development  in  a  manner  deemed  safe,  trustworthy,  and respectful  of  fundamental  rights  [1].

A  key  inspiration  in  the  EU's  approach  to  AI  regulation  has  been  its  own  GDPR. Introduced  in  2018,  the  GDPR  rapidly  became  a  global  benchmark  for  data  privacy, with  businesses  worldwide  scrambling  to  align  their  data  protection  practices  to  the new  standard.  The  EU  AI  Act,  although  broader  in  scope,  mirrors  the  GDPR  in  its extraterritorial  reach  and  risk-based  philosophy.  Much  like  the  GDPR,  it  establishes specific  obligations  and  potential  penalties,  incentivising  developers  and  deployers of  AI  to  incorporate  compliance  considerations  from  the  earliest  phases  of  design. In  essence,  the  success  of  the  GDPR  in  raising  global  awareness  around  data  privacy reassured  EU  policymakers  that  well-calibrated  regulation  could  shape  international norms  and  support  innovation  within  the  EU  market  [2].

## Upholding Fundamental Rights

The  EU  places  great  emphasis  on  fundamental  rights  such  as  human  dignity,  privacy, and  non-discrimination.  Throughout  the  drafting  stages  of  the  EU  AI  Act,  policymakers  repeatedly  stressed  that  AI  must  operate  in  ways  that  uphold,  rather  than undermine,  these  values.  Issues  such  as  social  scoring,  the  use  of  manipulative  AI in  public  spaces,  and  the  deployment  of  real-time  facial  recognition  systems  have all been  highlighted  as  areas  too sensitive or  risky  to  leave  unregulated.  The  Act thus  includes  provisions  explicitly  prohibiting  certain  uses  of  AI  that  are  deemed incompatible  with  the  EU's  ethical  and  social  values  [3].

Despite  concerns  that  regulatory  controls  might  stifle  innovation,  the  EU's  stated aim  is  to  encourage  responsible  AI  development.  The  Act  endeavours  to  provide a  stable,  predictable  environment  in  which  companies  can  confidently  invest  in  AI solutions  without  the  threat  of  unpredictable  legal  complications.  This  sense  of  legal

certainty,  it  is  argued,  will  bolster  the  EU's  competitiveness  on  the  global  stage,  much as  the  GDPR  arguably  enhanced  consumer  trust  in  digital  services.  For  manufacturers and engineering firms alike, having consistent rules across the EU  can simplify compliance  compared  to  managing  a  patchwork  of  country-specific  requirements across  the  EU  27  members  [4].

## 1.3 Objectives of the EU AI Act

Given  this  backdrop,  the  EU  AI  Act  can  be  seen  as  resting  on  three  primary  objectives, each  of  which  underpins  the  central  provisions:

## 1. Fostering Trustworthy AI

The  legislation  introduces  a  structured  approach  to  risk  categorisation,  ensuring  that systems  most  likely  to  cause  harm,  be  it  physical,  psychological,  or  societal,  face the strictest requirements. By focusing on transparency, accountability, and data governance,  the  Act  aims  to  promote  responsible  design  choices  and  mitigate  the risks  of  untested  or  unscrutinised  algorithms.

## 2. Safeguarding Rights and Well-Being

In  line  with  the  EU's  human-centric  ethos,  the  Act  safeguards  citizens  from  opaque, manipulative,  or  dangerous  AI  deployments.  The  explicit  prohibition  of  certain  practices  like  subliminal  manipulation  and  social  scoring,  signals  that  not  all  technological capabilities  are  acceptable,  regardless  of  their  economic  potential.  Similarly,  highrisk  applications  (for  instance,  in  employment  or  healthcare)  must  meet  stringent guidelines  to  ensure  individuals'  rights  and  well-being  remain  paramount.

## 3. Championing Innovation, Competitiveness, and Growth

The  EU  AI  Act  is not conceived  merely  as  a  protective measure;  it also aims  to create the conditions  in which  innovation  can  flourish. Clear responsibilities and shared  standards  offer  a  more  predictable  environment,  encouraging  investment.  It is  believed  that  a  robust  regulatory  framework  will  assure  consumers  and  business partners  that AI-driven  products  are  safe  and  reliable,  leading  to  higher  adoption rates.

## 1.4 Scope and Applicability

One  distinctive  aspect  of  the  EU  AI  Act  which  mirrors  the  GDPR  is  its  extraterritorial scope.  The  EU  AI  Act's  reach  extends  beyond  the  geographical  boundaries  of  the European  Union.  It  applies  to  providers  who  place  AI  systems  on  the  EU  market, irrespective  of  their  establishment  or  location.  Furthermore,  the  Act  is  applicable  to providers  and  deployers  located  outside  the  EU  if  the  output  produced  by  their  AI

systems  is  used  within  the  EU.  This  broad  scope  explicitly  includes  product  manufacturers  based  outside  the  EU  who  place  AI  systems  on  the  EU  market  together  with their  products.  Therefore,  non-EU  engineering  firms  and  manufacturers  intending  to offer  AI-powered  products  or  whose  AI-driven  processes  generate  outputs  used  in the  EU  must  comply  with  the  regulations  outlined  in  the  EU  AI  Act.

## Who Must Comply?

In  essence,  three  categories  of  actors  are  expected  to  comply  with  the  EU  AI  Act:

## 1. Provider

The  entity  (e.g.  a  manufacturer  or  developer)  that  places  an  AI  system  on  the  EU market  or puts it into service  under its  name  or  trademark.  For  a  manufacturing company,  if  you  develop  or  integrate  an  AI  component  into  your  product  and  sell  it, you  are  the  provider.

## 2. Deployer (User)

The  organisation  or  person  using  an  AI  system  under  their  authority.  In  manufacturing,  a  deployer  might  be  a  factory  owner  using  an  AI  tool  in  production.

## 3. Importer

An  EU-based  intermediary  that  places  a  non-EU  provider's  AI  system  on  the  EU market.  For  example,  if  a  non-European  company's  AI-equipped  machine  is  sold  in Europe  via  a  local  agent,  that  agent  is  the  importer  with  compliance  responsibilities.

## 4. Distributor

Other  intermediaries  in  the  supply  chain  making  the  AI  system  available  in  the  EU, who  are  not  the  provider  or  importer.

These  definitions  matter  because  obligations  under  the  Act  differ  for  providers, deployers,  importers,  and  distributors.  A  single  manufacturing  firm  might  even  play multiple  roles  (e.g.  developing  an  AI  system  as  a  provider  and  also  deploying  AI internally  as  a  user).  Understanding  these  roles  clarifies  who  must  do  what  to  comply.

## Territorial Extent

The territorial scope covers all EU Member  States. Additionally, if a non-EU company  places  AI  technology  on  the  EU  market,  either  in  physical  goods  or  as software  services,  it  also  falls  under  the  Act.  This  means  a  robotics  firm  in  Japan,  a healthcare  AI  company  in  the  United  States,  or  a  predictive  analytics  vendor  in  India must  ensure  their  offerings  meet  the  Act's  obligations  if  they  have  EU  customers.

## Material Scope: Defining AI Systems

The  Act  adopts  a  broad  definition  of  AI,  covering  an  extensive  range  of  software that  can  infer,  predict,  or  decide  based  on  input  data.  Whether  an  algorithm  employs classical  statistical  methods,  machine  learning,  or  symbolic  reasoning,  it  can  qualify as  AI  under  this  law.  This  inclusivity  ensures  that  new,  emerging  AI  approaches  do not  slip  through  regulatory  gaps  simply  because  they  do  not  fit  a  narrow  description.

Manufacturers  utilising  automation,  data  analytics,  or  algorithmic  decision-making in  their  production  lines  must  evaluate  whether  they  fall  within  the  Act's  domain.

## 1.5 Key Provisions and Structure

At  a  structural  level,  the  EU  AI  Act  is  divided  into  several  chapters  and  articles,  each addressing  different  aspects:

## · Risk Classification (Articles 5-8)

This  section  outlines  the  four  risk  tiers  (unacceptable,  high,  limited,  and  minimal), detailing  which  AI  applications  are  banned  outright  and  which  require  higher  levels of  scrutiny.

## · Obligations for High-Risk AI (Articles 9-16)

Here, the Act sets requirements around data governance, transparency, human oversight,  accuracy,  and  robustness.  Any  system  deemed  high  risk  must  undergo conformity  assessments  before  it  can  be  deployed.

## · Transparency Obligations for Certain AI (Article 52)

For  systems  interacting  directly  with  humans,  such  as  chatbots  or  AI  that  generates synthetic  media,  transparency  mandates  ensure  users  are  informed  they  are  engaging with  AI.

## · Governance and Enforcement Mechanisms (Articles 60-71)

These  articles  detail  how  national  authorities,  notified  bodies,  and  EU  institutions will  coordinate  to  enforce  the  Act,  including  penalties  for  non-compliance.

Manufacturers  need  to  be  particularly mindful  of the high-risk provisions. AI systems  in assembly  lines,  industrial robots,  or  safety-monitoring  solutions  often carry potentially serious implications for worker safety and product integrity. Consequently,  such  systems  are  frequently  designated  as  high  risk,  bringing  extra responsibilities  around  data  validation,  documentation,  and  monitoring.

## 1.6 The Timeline for Implementation

Much  like  the  GDPR's  staggered  approach,  the  EU  AI  Act  foresees  a  phased  implementation  to give organisations time to adjust. Although  precise  dates may  shift slightly based on legislative processes and final approvals, the general schedule includes:

## 1. Official Publication and Entry into Force

Shortly  after  the  text  is  published  in  the  Official  Journal  of  the  European  Union,  the regulation  formally  'enters  into  force,'  generally  a  few  weeks  from  publication.

## 2. Grace Period

There  is  an  interval  (often  ranging  from  12  to  24  months)  before  many  of  the  Act's provisions  become  fully  enforceable.  This  grace  period  allows  businesses  time  to understand  the  Act  and  begin  aligning  systems  and  procedures.

## 3. Full Applicability

By  a  specific  date,  all  or  most  obligations  come  into  effect.  High-risk  AI  systems will  typically  have  earlier  deadlines  for  conformity  assessments,  given  their  potential impact  on  safety  and  rights.

## 4. Further Stages

Certain  provisions  may  come  into  play  over  additional  time  frames-for  instance, rules  specific  to  'general-purpose  AI,'  if  included  in  final  texts,  or  new  appendices that  list  specific  technologies  subject  to  mandatory  risk  assessments.

For  manufacturers,  this  timeline  underscores  the  importance  of  proactive  efforts. Waiting  until  the  last  moment  could  result  in  non-compliant  products,  market  entry delays,  or  the  forced  withdrawal  of  AI  systems  from  critical  industrial  settings.

The  EU  AI  Act's  implementation  timeline  is  designed  to  give  businesses  time  to adapt:

July  2024:  Published  in  the  Official  Journal.

August  2024:  The  Act  enters  into  force,  establishing  its  legal  framework.

February 2025: Obligations for prohibited AI systems and basic compliance requirements  begin.

August  2025:  General-purpose  AI  models  must  comply  with  specific  provisions.

August  2026:  Most  obligations,  including  those  for  high-risk  AI  systems,  become enforceable.

August 2027: Final provisions for certain applications, including high-risk systems  in  Annex  I,  take  effect.

While  the  staggered  timeline  provides  some  flexibility,  businesses  must  act  early  to address  high-risk  systems,  which  often  require  detailed  assessments,  documentation, and  in-market  monitoring  [5-7].

## 1.7 Comparison with the GDPR

Drawing  a  parallel  between  the  EU  AI  Act  and  the  GDPR  is  inevitable,  as  the  latter has  already  transformed  how  the  world  approaches  data  privacy  and  protection.  While both  pieces  of  legislation  share  a  similar  ambition  to  protect  fundamental  rights  and encourage  responsible  innovation,  they  differ  in  their  scope  and  operational  details.
